I thought I had an AI problem.  
Turns out I had a review problem.

For months, I was generating content, strategy notes, and task plans with AI all week… then wondering why by Friday everything felt noisy and inconsistent. I was producing more, but trusting less.

What changed: I stopped “prompt tweaking daily” and built one fixed weekly review loop on Sunday night.

I start by collecting 7 days of outputs into one doc: shipped posts, drafts I abandoned, AI-generated plans, and actual business results (replies, signups, conversion events). No cherry-picking. If it was produced, it goes in.

Then I run a 3-pass review:

Pass 1 is signal tagging (20 minutes).  
I mark each output as one of three: “kept,” “edited heavily,” or “discarded.” If I had to rewrite more than ~40%, it counts as heavily edited. This instantly shows where AI is helping vs creating cleanup work.

Pass 2 is gap mapping (25 minutes).  
For every heavily edited/discarded item, I write one short reason: wrong audience, weak specificity, fake confidence, no CTA, bad structure, etc. After 4 weeks, patterns became obvious—most failures came from vague context, not model quality.

Pass 3 is workflow patching (15 minutes).  
I only allow myself to change three things for next week:  
- one input rule (what context must be provided)  
- one output rule (what format/quality gate is required)  
- one rejection rule (when to regenerate immediately)

Example patch from last week: I forced every draft to include one measurable claim and one real execution detail before approval. If either was missing, regenerate. No exceptions.

Measured impact after 5 weeks:  
- “heavily edited” rate dropped from 58% to 21%  
- average editing time per post dropped from 34 min to 11 min  
- weekly publishing consistency improved from 3.2 days/week to 5.8 days/week

The contrarian part: consistency did not come from better prompts. It came from a stricter review system that teaches your workflow what “useful” means.

This works if you ship every week and can compare output against real outcomes. If you publish once in a while, or you don’t track what happened after posting, this will feel like admin overhead.

I’m not done yet. Next round, I’m adding a “false-positive” check: outputs that look polished but produce weak business response. That’s where hidden quality debt still lives.

我会按三步推进：先把输入和验收写清，再跑最小闭环，最后只保留有效动作并沉淀成模板。
这套方法更适合高频、可验收的重复任务；一次性探索型问题不建议硬套。
